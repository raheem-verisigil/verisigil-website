What Happens If Your AI Agent Is High-Risk Under the EU AI Act?

By Raheem Larry Babatunde · VeriSigil AI · May 7, 2026 · 6 minutes
⚠️ August 1, 2026: High-risk AI agents must be fully compliant or face fines up to €30M. Here is what high-risk actually means and what you must do.

The EU AI Act's most demanding requirements apply to "high-risk" AI systems. If your AI agent falls into this category, the obligations are substantial — and August 1, 2026 is a hard enforcement date.

Which AI Agents Are Classified as High-Risk?

An AI agent is classified as high-risk if it operates in one of these areas:

  • Biometric identification and categorisation — any agent that identifies people using biometric data
  • Critical infrastructure — agents managing electricity, water, gas, transport, or digital networks
  • Education and training — agents that assess students, determine admission, or evaluate academic performance
  • Employment and worker management — agents used in hiring, promotion, termination, or task allocation
  • Essential private and public services — credit scoring, benefits eligibility, emergency services dispatch
  • Law enforcement — agents used for risk assessment, crime prediction, or evidence analysis
  • Border control — agents for visa assessment, asylum processing, or immigration decisions
  • Justice administration — agents that help apply laws or make judicial decisions

What High-Risk Classification Actually Requires

The moment your agent is high-risk, the following become legal obligations — not optional best practices.

1. Cryptographic Identity (Mandatory)

Your agent must have a verifiable identity. Regulators must be able to confirm who built it, who deployed it, and that it has not been tampered with. A cryptographic passport — like a W3C DID signed with Ed25519 — satisfies this requirement. A generic API key does not.

2. Immutable Audit Trail (Mandatory)

Every decision your agent makes must be recorded in a tamper-proof log. The audit trail must capture what data was used, what the agent decided, and when. Retroactive alteration of logs is prohibited under the Act.

3. Human Oversight (Mandatory)

A designated human must be able to monitor, override, and shut down the agent. This oversight must be technically implemented — not just stated in a policy document. The agent must be designed so that human intervention is always possible.

4. Conformity Assessment (Mandatory for Highest-Risk)

For certain high-risk categories — biometric identification, law enforcement, critical infrastructure — a third-party conformity assessment is required before deployment. You cannot self-certify.

5. Registration in EU Database (Mandatory)

High-risk AI systems must be registered in the EU's public AI database before deployment. This registration requires your organisation's legal name, address, a description of the system, and its risk classification.

The Fines Are Real

Non-compliance with high-risk AI requirements carries fines of up to €30 million or 6% of global annual turnover — whichever is higher. For enterprises with global revenue, 6% of turnover is the more significant number.

What to Do If Your Agent Is High-Risk

You have 87 days. In order of priority:

  • Issue a cryptographic passport for your agent — satisfies identity and transparency requirements
  • Enable signed audit logging — satisfies traceability requirements
  • Document your human oversight workflow — satisfies oversight requirements
  • Register in the EU AI database — legally required before deployment
  • Commission a conformity assessment if required for your specific use case
